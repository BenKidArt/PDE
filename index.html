Chapter One

The Man Who Came in From the Cold

In December 1961, a Soviet KGB officer named Anatoliy Mikhailovich Golitsyn walked into the American Embassy in Helsinki and defected. He came bearing something more dangerous than weapons: knowledge of the plan.

Golitsyn had worked at the very top levels of Soviet intelligence. He knew how the KGB thought — not just operationally, but strategically. He understood that the Soviet Union played a very long game, measured not in months but in decades.

He cooperated with Western intelligence for years. But he grew increasingly frustrated. The agencies believed what they wanted to believe. They were being deceived, and they could not see it.

So he wrote it down. In 1984: "New Lies for Old." In 1995: "The Perestroika Deception." Two books. One warning. Read by very few. Dismissed by many. Vindicated — he would argue — by history.

Soviet Intelligence Officer · Turned Witness

"The 'liberalization' in the Soviet bloc countries will be calculated, controlled, and deceptive. It will be designed to achieve the disorientation of the West."

— Anatoliy Golitsyn, "New Lies for Old," 1984 — written seven years before the Soviet "collapse"

Chapter Two

The Great Performance

The Bear Performs — The West Applauds

In 1985, Mikhail Gorbachev took power and announced two revolutionary words: Perestroika (restructuring) and Glasnost (openness). The Western world erupted in celebration.

The Berlin Wall fell in 1989. The Soviet Union dissolved in 1991. Leaders were given Nobel Peace Prizes. Champagne was uncorked. The Cold War, they declared, was over.

Golitsyn watched from the sidelines, increasingly alarmed. Because he had written — in 1984, before Gorbachev, before glasnost, before any of it — that exactly this would happen. A controlled, theatrical liberalisation, designed to exhaust Western vigilance.

The plan, he said, was not to defeat the West militarily. It was to make the West defeat itself — by convincing it the enemy was gone, causing it to disarm economically, militarily, and morally, while the strategic apparatus quietly continued underground.

Yuri Bezmenov: The Subverter's Confession

If Golitsyn warned about the geopolitical deception, Yuri Bezmenov explained the psychological machinery behind it. A KGB propaganda specialist who defected to Canada in 1970, Bezmenov spent the rest of his life trying to explain what his former employers were doing to Western minds.

His most famous interview, given in 1984 under the name "Tomas Schuman," described a four-stage process he called Ideological Subversion — a slow, deliberate rot of a society from within, requiring no bullets at all.

"The main emphasis of the KGB is not in the area of intelligence at all," he said. "Only about fifteen percent of KGB resources are spent on espionage. The other 85% is a slow process we call Ideological Subversion or Active Measures."

The goal was not to convert Westerners to communism. It was far more subtle: to make them unable to process true information. To fill universities, media, and institutions with people who would reflexively defend a worldview hostile to their own civilization — and who would be genuinely incapable of recognising the manipulation even when shown evidence of it.

The Mind Is The Battlefield

"You cannot change their mind even if you expose them to authentic information. The process of demoralisation is complete and irreversible."

— Yuri Bezmenov, interview with G. Edward Griffin, 1984

Oleg Gordievsky: The Man Inside

KGB Colonel · MI6 Asset · Cold War's Greatest Spy

While Golitsyn and Bezmenov were defectors who came out from the cold, Oleg Gordievsky stayed in — operating for a decade as a British double agent inside the KGB while rising to become the KGB's London station chief.

Recruited by MI6 in 1974, Gordievsky passed some of the most sensitive Soviet intelligence of the Cold War era to Britain. He helped prevent what some believe was a genuine near-miss with nuclear war during Operation RYAN — a Soviet program that monitored NATO for signs of a surprise nuclear first strike. Soviet leaders, including an increasingly paranoid Kremlin, had convinced themselves the West was preparing to attack. Gordievsky told London how seriously Moscow took this threat — information that directly influenced President Reagan's approach.

In 1985, suspecting he had been betrayed, Gordievsky was summoned back to Moscow, interrogated and drugged. He never confessed. In a daring operation codenamed PIMLICO, MI6 smuggled him across the Finnish border in the boot of a car.

His testimony confirmed much of what Golitsyn had argued: the KGB's active measures were vast, coordinated, and targeted specifically at eroding Western unity and self-confidence.

The Epstein Network: Power, Blackmail & Elite Capture

This chapter covers documented facts about Jeffrey Epstein's criminal network, his death, and the unanswered questions surrounding his connections to intelligence agencies. It does not endorse specific unverified theories, but presents what is established and what remains unknown — because both matter.

Jeffrey Epstein was a financier of murky origins who built a social network of extraordinary reach — connecting politicians, royalty, scientists, intelligence officials, and billionaires across decades. He was convicted of soliciting prostitution from a minor in 2008, received a strikingly lenient sentence, and was arrested again in 2019 on federal sex trafficking charges involving dozens of minors.

On August 10, 2019, Epstein was found dead in his cell at the Metropolitan Correctional Center in New York. The official ruling was suicide by hanging. The circumstances were — and remain — extraordinary: surveillance cameras malfunctioned, two guards both fell asleep or were inattentive, and Epstein had reportedly been taken off suicide watch despite a previous incident. The medical examiner's ruling of suicide was contested by a forensic pathologist hired by the family.

What concerns analysts who study influence operations is not Epstein as an isolated criminal, but Epstein as a system. The documented evidence — flight logs, property records, court testimony — shows a man who placed powerful individuals in compromising positions and maintained extraordinary access to the corridors of global power for decades.

Former Israeli Prime Minister Ehud Barak was photographed visiting Epstein's New York apartment. British Prince Andrew settled a civil lawsuit with one of Epstein's trafficking victims. Les Wexner, the retail billionaire who gave Epstein his primary source of wealth, eventually cut ties and said he had been "misused." Former President Bill Clinton flew on Epstein's private jet multiple times. Former President Donald Trump was photographed socialising with Epstein and had to distance himself from the relationship.

Intelligence analysts have long noted that running operations targeting powerful individuals through sexual compromise — known as a "honey trap" or kompromat operation — is a cornerstone of both KGB and Mossad tradecraft. The World Today: Is The Long Game Still Running?

Read Golitsyn's warnings. Read Bezmenov's stages. Look at the world in 2025 and ask: does this resemble what they described?

In the West, institutional trust has collapsed to historic lows. Media is fragmented. Universities are contested battlegrounds. Political systems across Europe and North America are experiencing deep polarisation. People cannot agree on basic facts. Allies squabble. NATO is under pressure from within as much as from without.

Russia under Vladimir Putin — a former KGB officer — has annexed Crimea (2014), invaded Ukraine (2022), and conducted documented influence operations across virtually every Western democracy. The Internet Research Agency and GRU military intelligence have run active campaigns seeding division, amplifying extremes, and undermining confidence in democratic processes.

China's United Front Work Department — a body devoted to overseas influence operations — operates at a scale that dwarfs anything the KGB achieved. Academic infiltration, technology theft, elite networking, and economic leverage are deployed simultaneously.

Meanwhile, the domestic politics of Western nations have grown stranger. Conspiracy theories have proliferated — some manufactured, some organic, some deliberately seeded. It becomes harder to distinguish real corruption from manufactured distraction. This confusion itself is a feature, not a bug, of a successful influence operation.

Conclusion

What Thinking Children Do

This is a serious story. It does not have a happy ending yet — because the story is not finished. But it does have a clear lesson.

Three men — Golitsyn, Bezmenov, Gordievsky — dedicated their lives to warning the West about something they had seen from the inside. They disagreed on details. They came from different backgrounds. But they agreed on the core: a long-range strategic deception was underway, targeting not the military defences of Western nations, but their minds.

The Epstein network — whatever its origins — demonstrated that the most powerful lever of control is not military force. It is the behaviour of powerful people, and the information about that behaviour held by those who wish to use it.

The world today presents a citizen with a difficult problem: how to think clearly in an environment saturated with manipulation, disinformation, and deliberately engineered outrage. This is not an accident. It is, at least in part, a design.

So what do thinking children do? They read primary sources. They ask who benefits. They resist the comfort of simple narratives — both the narrative that says "everything is fine" and the narrative that says "everything is a conspiracy." They hold uncertainty without collapsing into paranoia or complacency.

They remember that the most effective deceptions are not the ones that are completely false. They are the ones that are mostly true, with a crucial lie at the centre.
